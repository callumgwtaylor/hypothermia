Hypothermia

2 Introduction

Accidental Hypothermia (AH) is defined as “an involuntary drop in core body temperature to less than 35°C” (95°F).1,2 This can be “primary hypothermia”, where the body’s heat production mechanisms are overcome by excessive cold, or “secondary hypothermia” where concurrent illness impairs our ability to maintain normothermia (this can occur in warm temperatures)1 The lowest temperature a human is known to have survived AH was 13.7°C (this patient survived after 6.5 hours of CPR!)3

Multiple patient factors increase a person’s risk of hypothermia, these could be expedition specific including: lack of cold acclimatisation, lack of insulation, exhaustion, trauma, calorie deficit, and sleep deprivation. Alcohol and drug ingestion also increase risk of AH.2,3

Whilst those aged 30-49 are the most frequently treated for hypothermia, death from hypothermia is most common in those over 65 years. The elderly can be more at risk from chronic disease, and medications that impair their ability to compensate in cold environments.2,3

Environmental factors that increase risk of hypothermia include: the temperature gradient, the nature of the cold medium (air, water, snow, or ice), wind speed, or rough water.3

Accidental Hypothermia is not limited to remote or polar environments, 1500 patients in the US yearly have hypothermia on their death certificate, and it is often found in patients in urban settings.1

2.0.1 Key Points

  • Accidental Hypothermia is an involuntary core body temperature below 35°C
  • Can be Primary or Secondary Hypothermia
  • Rate of cooling can be influenced by patient and environmental factors
