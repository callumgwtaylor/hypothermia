Hypothermia

9 CPR/ALS

In stage IV hypothermia (the arrested patient), some modifications are made to standard ALS. As patients with hypothermia can be significantly bradycardic it is recommended to check for signs of life for 60 seconds rather than the standard 10.1

The European Resuscitation Council recommend the following changes from standard ALS guidance in 2015:

  • After three shocks, further shocks should be witheld until core temperature is greater than 30°C
  • The use of adrenaline should be witheld until core temperature is greater than 30°C
  • The period of time between adrenaline administration should double until core temperature is greater than 35°C

2

In hypothermic cardiac arrest, a patient is unlikely to regain circulation until rewarmed.6 When cooled, due to the decrease in cerebral O2 requirements, the brain can tolerate CPR for significantly longer (hours).1 Brain O2 consumption drops by 6% every 1C drop in core temperature, meaning that at 18°C the brain tolerates cardiac arrest 10 times longer than at 37°C3. As such we often hear the adage “they aren’t dead until they’re warm and dead”

In a prehospital setting, arrested patients should have core temperature monitoring, but heat delivery should only be to maintain current temperature rather than rewarming, until the patient is delivered at a centre capable of providing ECLS. This is to maximise the chance of preserving good neurological function.3 In addition, mechanical CPR devices are recommended for prolonged evacuations, and if consistent CPR is not possible intermittent CPR may be useful.3

If ECLS is not possible, as pleural lavage may impair chest compressions, another strategy would be rewarming with external heat delivery, plus bladder lavage through a 3-way catheter, or peritoneal lavage. This recommendation from International Commission for Mountain Emergency Medicine is based on expert opinion, as no good trial evidence for non-ECLS rewarming in arrest exists.3

9.1 Terminating CPR

If you aren’t dead until you’re warm and dead, how warm is enough? Current recommendation is that temperatures between 32-35 without return of spontaneous circulation, should lead to consideration of stopping CPR/ECLS.3

Other triggers to consider ceasing resuscitation are:

  • Serum Potassium >8/>12 mmol/L
  • Obvious Fatal Injuries
  • Non-Compressible Chest
  • Signs of Anoxic Brain Injury
  • Other Identified Non Reversible Cause of Cardiac Arrest

1,2,3

A raised potassium is considered a marker of tissue hypoxia before cooling (suggesting a worse prognosis). The differing figures for serum potassium depend on the cause of hypothermic cardiac arrest (avalanche versus cold exposure). A serum potassium of >8 in an avalanche victim suggests a significant burial time with low likelihood of survival. A potassium of >12 has not been seen in a patient that survived hypothermic arrest. The highest survived potassium in accidental hypothermia was an infant with a serum potassium of 11.8.8 Whilst high potassium implies poor chances of survival, potassium below the threshold does not mean good chance of survival. In one study, out of 49 patients who did not survive hypothermic arrest, 44 of them had serum potassium levels of less than 12mmol/L.8

9.1.1 Key Points

  • Take 60 seconds rather than 10 when looking for signs of life in hypothermic patients
  • Stop shocking after three attempts in hypothermic cardiac arrest. Reattempt shocking when the patient is warmer than 30°C
  • Don’t give adrenaline until the patient is warmer than 30°C (double the interval between adrenaline until warmer than 35°C)
  • ECLS has significant evidence showing it’s benefit in hypothermic cardiac arrest
  • Hypothermic cardiac arrest resuscitation can be long, but a cold brain can tolerate this.
  • 32°C is warm enough to be “warm and dead”.
