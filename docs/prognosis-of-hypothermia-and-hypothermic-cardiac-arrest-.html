Hypothermia

11 Prognosis of hypothermia, and hypothermic cardiac arrest.

Patients with primary hypothermia and cardiac stability should have a 100% chance of neurologically intact survival when treated with active minimally invasive rewarming.1

Patients with hypothermia driven cardiac arrest treated with ECLS, have an estimated survival between 50-100%.1. Without ECLS these patients have an estimated survival of 37%.2

ECLS patients are more likely to survive when treated with VA-ECMO than with cardio-pulmonary bypass. One major factor here is the high percentage survival (100% in one trial) of post ROSC pulmonary oedema in VA-ECMO patients, something that is usually responsible for the highest proportion of deaths in post ROSC hypothermia patients.1,8,2

Survival of hypothermic cardiac arrest with good neurological outcome, in non avalanche patients, is estimated at 36%6 Including avalanches this percentage falls to 25%.6

Avalanche patients with out of hospital cardiac arrest have an estimated 8% chance of survival. One trial of cardiac arrest in avalanche patients found 37 cases, with only 2 surviving to a good neurological outcome and a normal life.6

Factors that appear to increase the chance of a good neurological outcome appear to be: - Non Avalanche Patients - Water Submersion For Shorter Periods of Time - Witnessed Cardiac Arrest - Lower Serum Potassium - Shorter Duration of CPR

6,8

Factors that increase the likelihood of a poor neurological outcome:

  • Avalanche Burial
  • Pathology Seen on CT Scanning

Factors that don’t affect the likelihood of a good neurological outcome:

  • Shockable/Non Shockable Rhythms (although in avalanche patients, VF/PEA have better outcomes than asystole)
  • ROSC Pre Hospital/ROSC In Hospital
  • Body Core Temperature

6,3

CT scanning of the head can show evidence of brain anoxia, as well as trauma.3 Serum potassium levels can be used as a marker of tissue hypoxia before arrest, and increased potassium correlates with brain anoxia.3 However the limitations of CT scanning must be recognised. One trial of CT scanning in the first 12 hours after cardiac arrest, found that 17% of patients who would survive with good neurological outcome had scans showing traditionally pessimistic signs such as “attenuation of grey/white matter or sulcal effacement”.6

11.0.1 Key Points

  • Using minimally invasive techniques to rewarm stable hypothermic patients provides an excellent chance of survival with good neurological outcome
  • Patients with primary hypothermia and cardiac arrest have approximately a 1/3 chance of survival with non-ECLS rewarming techniques, and a 50 to 100% chance of survival with ECLS.
  • VA-ECMO virtually eliminates the post ROSC major cause of death in hypothermia patients (post rewarming pulmonary oedema)
  • Predicting the chance of survival with good neurological outcome is difficult
  • Patients can tolerate cardiac arrest far longer than expected in normothermia
  • CT head findings to guide poor outcome after cardiac may be inaccurate
  • A period of observation over >48 hours is recommended by current guidance
