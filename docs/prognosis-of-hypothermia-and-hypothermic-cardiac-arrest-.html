Hypothermia

11 Prognosis of hypothermia, and hypothermic cardiac arrest.

Amongst patients with primary hypothermia and cardiac stability, the chance of a neurologically intact survival is close to 100% when treated with active minimally invasive rewarming.1

Survival in patients with hypothermia driven cardiac arrest, when treated with ECLS, is found to be between 50-100%.1. Without ECLS estimated survival drops to 37%.2

Looking at different forms of ECLS, patients are more likely to survive when treated with VA-ECMO than with cardio-pulmonary bypass. One major explanatory factor here is the high percentage of survival (100% in one trial) of post ROSC pulmonary oedema in VA-ECMO patients. Pulmonary oedema would usually be responsible for the highest proportion of deaths in post ROSC hypothermia patients.1,8,2

Beyond simply surviving cardiac arrest, good neurological outcome in non avalanche patients is estimated at 36%6 When avalanche victims are included this percentage falls to 25%.6

The prognosis of avalanche patients with out of hospital cardiac arrest is poor, with an 8% chance of survival. One study of cardiac arrest in avalanche patients identified 37 cases, with only 2 surviving to a good neurological outcome and a normal life.6

Factors that appear to increase the chance of a good neurological outcome include being a non avalanche patient, short period of water submersion, short duration of CPR, witnessed cardiac arrest, and low serum potassium.6,8 Whereas patients buried in avalanche, or with visible pathology on CT scan have poorer chance of a good outcome. Perhaps surprisingly both shockable and nonshockable rhythms have similar outcomes, when comparing VF and PEA. Although both have better neurological outcomes than an asystolic arrest. The core body temperature on rescue, and whether ROSC is pre-hospital or in hospital, do not affect the chance of a good outcome.6,3

CT scanning in potential hypothermic cardiac arrest can show evidence of brain anoxia, as well as trauma.3 Another more immediate investigation is serum potassium levels, which have been seen to correlate with brain anoxia.3 However the limitations of CT scanning must be recognised. One trial of CT scan in the first 12 hours after cardiac arrest, found that 17% of patients who would eventually survive with good neurological outcome had initial scans showing traditionally pessimistic signs such as “attenuation of grey/white matter or sulcal effacement”.6

11.0.1 Key Points

  • Using minimally invasive techniques to rewarm stable hypothermic patients provides an excellent chance of survival with good neurological outcome
  • Patients with primary hypothermia and cardiac arrest have approximately a 1/3 chance of survival with non-ECLS rewarming techniques, and a 50 to 100% chance of survival with ECLS.
  • VA-ECMO virtually eliminates the post ROSC major cause of death in hypothermia patients (post rewarming pulmonary oedema)
  • Predicting the chance of survival with good neurological outcome is difficult
  • Patients can tolerate cardiac arrest far longer than expected in normothermia
  • CT head findings to guide poor outcome after cardiac may be inaccurate
  • A period of observation over >48 hours is recommended by current guidance
